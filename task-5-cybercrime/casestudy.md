# Case Study: The "Request Money" Trap (UPI Fraud)

### What exactly is this?
Imagine you're selling an old textbook or a lab coat on a college WhatsApp group. A "buyer" messages you instantly, acting super eager. This is the start of UPI Fraud. It’s not a high-tech hack; it’s a psychological trick. [cite_start]Attackers exploit the simple misunderstanding of how UPI apps like Google Pay or PhonePe actually work to steal money in real-time.

### How the Trap Sprung (Step-by-Step)
1. **The Friendly Buyer**: A person contacts you posing as a student or a genuine buyer from a site like OLX. 
2. **Building Trust**: They send a fake screenshot showing they’ve already "sent" the money to your account, but claim it's "on hold".
3. **The "Collect" Trick**: They send you a notification on your UPI app. [cite_start]They tell you over the phone, "Just tap 'Pay' and enter your PIN to authorize the deposit into your account".
4. **The Instant Loss**: The second you enter your PIN, you aren't receiving money—you are authorizing a payment *to* them. [cite_start]Your bank balance drops instantly, and the "buyer" blocks your number.

### Who is at Risk?
We—first-year students—are often the primary targets. We are comfortable with tech but often move too fast. [cite_start]Whether we are paying hostel deposits or selling used gear, we are vulnerable if we don't remember the golden rule: **You never need a PIN to receive money**.

### The Aftermath
The consequences go beyond the empty bank account. There is a heavy sense of "how could I be so stupid?" which leads to many students not reporting the crime out of embarrassment. It’s a stressful cycle that can be broken simply by staying informed.

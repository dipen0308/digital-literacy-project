# Case Study: The "Request Money" Trap (UPI Fraud)

### What exactly is this?
Imagine you're selling an old textbook or a lab coat on a college WhatsApp group. A "buyer" messages you instantly, acting super eager. This is the start of UPI Fraud. It’s not a high-tech hack; it’s a psychological trick. [cite_start]Attackers exploit the simple misunderstanding of how UPI apps like Google Pay or PhonePe actually work to steal money in real-time[cite: 77, 78].

### How the Trap Sprung (Step-by-Step)
1. [cite_start]**The Friendly Buyer**: A person contacts you posing as a student or a genuine buyer from a site like OLX[cite: 78, 79]. 
2. [cite_start]**Building Trust**: They send a fake screenshot showing they’ve already "sent" the money to your account, but claim it's "on hold"[cite: 78].
3. **The "Collect" Trick**: They send you a notification on your UPI app. [cite_start]They tell you over the phone, "Just tap 'Pay' and enter your PIN to authorize the deposit into your account"[cite: 78].
4. **The Instant Loss**: The second you enter your PIN, you aren't receiving money—you are authorizing a payment *to* them. [cite_start]Your bank balance drops instantly, and the "buyer" blocks your number[cite: 78].

### Who is at Risk?
[cite_start]We—first-year students—are often the primary targets[cite: 78]. We are comfortable with tech but often move too fast. [cite_start]Whether we are paying hostel deposits or selling used gear, we are vulnerable if we don't remember the golden rule: **You never need a PIN to receive money**[cite: 78].

### The Aftermath
[cite_start]The consequences go beyond the empty bank account[cite: 78]. [cite_start]There is a heavy sense of "how could I be so stupid?" which leads to many students not reporting the crime out of embarrassment. It’s a stressful cycle that can be broken simply by staying informed.
